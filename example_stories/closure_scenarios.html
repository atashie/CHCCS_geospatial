From Demographics to Movement

The previous stories examined environmental conditions and who lives where. This story examines what happens when children must move.

School closure redirects hundreds of students, increasing travel times and road congestion. Only three schools are considered for closure — Ephesus, Glenwood, and Seawell. We analyze what each closure means for transportation.

2

How We Measure Impact

For every 100-meter pixel in the district, Dijkstra’s shortest-path algorithm computes driving time to each school. When a school closes, students route to their next-nearest school. The difference = extra travel time.

Traffic volume is estimated by distributing Census child counts (dasymetric allocation) to each pixel, then tracing each pixel’s route to its nearest school. The number of children per road segment becomes our traffic proxy.

Young Children by Nearest School

Ephesus has 634 children under 5 in its drive-time zone — compared to Seawell’s 123. These are future kindergarteners.

Closing Ephesus removes capacity where future enrollment demand is highest. This disparity exists because Ephesus serves the most population-dense area of the district.

Key finding: Ephesus has ~5x more children under 5 than Seawell (634 vs 123). Closing it removes elementary capacity precisely where future demand is greatest.

The School Desert Scenario

Now imagine closing both Ephesus and Glenwood.

This removes two schools from the most population-dense part of the eastern district. The remaining nearby school — Rashkis — is not only climate-vulnerable (adjacent to FEMA AE 100-year flood zone) but also has limited traffic access.

Meadowmont Lane is essentially the only vehicular corridor, creating extreme congestion if hundreds of additional families must funnel through it.

9

Rashkis: Flood Risk & Access

Rashkis sits adjacent to a FEMA AE flood zone (7.1% of property in 100-year zone, 4.8% in 500-year). Its road access is constrained to a single corridor.

If eastern schools close and Rashkis absorbs their students, you get a flood-vulnerable school overwhelmed with traffic on a single access corridor. This is not just inconvenience — it is a safety concern.

10

What the Data Shows

Three key findings from the closure analysis:

  1. Ephesus closure is more disruptive: 505 affected road segments vs 222 for Seawell; ~5x more young children in its zone
  2. Traffic burden falls on community school families, not already-bussed LEAP students at Seawell
  3. Closing eastern schools creates a school desert in the most population-dense area, overloading a flood-vulnerable school with constrained access
