# Example Stories

Editorial-style scrollytelling pages that compare specific schools using the same data and methodology as the neutral analysis pages in `src/`.

These are **editorial** (they make arguments based on the data) as opposed to the methodology walkthroughs in `src/` which are **neutral** (they explain how the analysis works).

## Environmental Conditions

**File:** `environmental_conditions_story.py`
**Output:** `environmental_conditions.html`

The first in an Ephesus-focused editorial series. Presents objective environmental/geographic data for all 11 elementary schools, making the case that environmental factors should inform closure decisions. Precedes the demographics story.

Three environmental dimensions:

1. **Flood Risk** — FEMA flood zone overlap with school properties (only FPG and Rashkis affected)
2. **Traffic Air Pollution (TRAP)** — modeled exposure from nearby roads with tree canopy mitigation
3. **Urban Heat Island (UHI)** — heat vulnerability proxy from ESA WorldCover land cover

### Color Scheme

- **Ephesus** = red (`#C62828`)
- **Glenwood** = green (`#2E7D32`)
- **Seawell** = blue (`#1565C0`)
- **FPG** = amber (`#FF8F00`)
- **Other schools** = gray (`#888888`)

### Usage

```bash
python example_stories/environmental_conditions_story.py
python example_stories/environmental_conditions_story.py --cache-only
```

- TRAP and UHI raster grids rendered as base64 PNG image overlays

## Ephesus vs. Seawell Comparison

**File:** `ephesus_seawell_story.py`
**Output:** `ephesus_seawell_comparison.html`

A data-driven comparison of Ephesus Elementary and Seawell Elementary across four dimensions:

1. **Socioeconomic Status** — poverty counts, affordable housing, drive-time vs. attendance-zone demographics
2. **Race/Ethnicity** — dot-density mapping, minority counts, Hispanic population
3. **Age Distribution** — young children (0–4), elementary age (5–9), future enrollment demand
4. **Real Estate Trends** — MLS home sales counts, median prices by zone (2023–2025)

### Color Scheme

- **Ephesus** = red (`#C62828`)
- **Seawell** = blue (`#1565C0`)
- **Combined/actual values** = purple (`#6A1B9A`)
- **Other schools** = gray (`#cccccc`)

### Narrative Structure

The story opens with **methodology first** (steps 0–3): introducing the district, explaining attendance zones vs. drive-time zones, then focusing on Ephesus and Seawell. This ensures readers understand the zone concepts before seeing school comparisons.

All comparisons lead with **counts over percentages** — real human impact matters more than rates when zone populations differ significantly. Seawell's attendance zone is geographically large but sparsely populated in its outer reaches.

**Drive-time zones are the primary lens** since any closure triggers rezoning. Attendance zones are shown as dashed overlays for comparison.

### Key Analytical Insight

Seawell's attendance zone appears economically vulnerable, but drive-time analysis reveals much of that vulnerable population is actually closer to other schools. Meanwhile, Ephesus serves a larger, more diverse population with more young children and nearly 3x the affordable housing units.

## School Closure Scenarios

**File:** `closure_scenarios_story.py`
**Output:** `closure_scenarios.html`

Third and final story in the Ephesus-focused editorial series. Examines student movement under closure scenarios — traffic redistribution, transportation costs, and the school desert risk of closing eastern schools.

Three analytical dimensions:

1. **Traffic Redistribution** — road segments gaining/losing children when a school closes (Seawell vs. Ephesus)
2. **Drive-Time Increase** — heatmap of additional commute time under closure
3. **School Desert Risk** — what happens when both eastern schools close, overloading flood-vulnerable Rashkis

### Color Scheme

- **Ephesus** = red (`#C62828`)
- **Glenwood** = green (`#2E7D32`)
- **Seawell** = blue (`#1565C0`)
- **FPG** = amber (`#FF8F00`)
- **Other schools** = gray (`#888888`)

### Key Analytical Insights

- Ephesus closure affects 505 road segments vs. 222 for Seawell (2.3x more widespread)
- Ephesus has 634 children under 5 in its drive-time zone vs. 123 for Seawell (~5x more)
- Closing both Ephesus and Glenwood creates a school desert in the most population-dense area, funneling traffic to flood-vulnerable Rashkis
